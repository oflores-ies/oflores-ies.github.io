Conclusions

When analyzing the project as a whole, it is clear that what began as a massive and complex R and Python database has ended up providing a very accurate snapshot of air quality in Barcelona. The process of cleaning the data from Meteocat and the XPVCA network, together with the use of SIVIC codes and APIs from the Catalan government, has made it possible to transform abstract figures into pollution patterns that are now fully visible.

It is striking to see how daily human activity, especially commuting to school or work, is so dramatically reflected in the $NO_2$ graphs. The visualizations literally map the city's emissions according to traffic schedules, showing that pollution peaks are not random but follow the rhythm of residents' lives.

Looking at the final results, it is clear that although the levels of certain gases have fluctuated over the years, peak levels during rush hour remain a critical constant, especially in high-density areas such as the Sants district. The use of visualization tools such as the openair library has been essential in converting technical data tables into heat maps and graphs that are understandable to any citizen.

In conclusion, this study demonstrates that technology and programming are essential tools for diagnosing real urban problems. The analysis reveals that the quality of the air we breathe is not a random factor, but rather the direct result of the mobility patterns and lifestyle habits that are maintained in the city day after day.
